Protocol
Proof of Will
The three formulas that make participation irreversible
Most blockchain protocols reward computation — proof of work — or capital — proof of stake. YourMine rewards something different: will. The deliberate choice to participate, to wait, to contribute quality over quantity. Three formulas encode this intent into the protocol.
I. Mining — the patience formula
S · t^α ───────────────────────────── [ln(A^β(1−T) + C)]^γ
S · t^α — burn amount × time elapsed. The longer you wait after burning, the more you earn. Time is in the numerator. Rushing is penalized.
ln(A^β(1−T) + C)^γ — protocol age in the denominator. As YourMine matures, the same action yields less. Early participants are structurally rewarded. Anti-inflation is not a policy — it is a mathematical consequence.
T — the patience rate — chosen freely by the user between 0 and 1. It modulates how age affects your reward. This is the "will" in Proof of Will: you declare your temporal commitment, and the protocol encodes it permanently.
The deep property: this formula cannot be gamed by speed. The only variables you control are S (how much you burn) and T (how patient you declare yourself). Everything else — time, protocol age — is objective. You cannot fake will.
II. Permission score — the quality gate
(score_now + 1) / (laps_now + 1) > (score_last + 1) / (laps_last + 1)
To publish a new file in the registry, your current ratio must exceed your previous ratio. The +1 is a Laplace smoothing — your first publication is always allowed. After that, each new contribution must improve your average.
Anti-spam by construction — you cannot flood the registry with mediocre files. Each new sphere must be better than your average to be admitted.
No subjective curation — quality is not judged by a committee or an algorithm. It is judged by the network, which votes with score. The gate is mathematical, not editorial.
Updates are free — improving an existing sphere requires only ownership verification. The quality gate applies only to new files, not to iterations. Improvement is always rewarded.
III. Rank — the frozen reputation
rank = score / laps
Score is frozen at merge time. Rank is computed from this frozen score, divided by the number of publications. It never changes retroactively. No algorithm boosts new files. No editorial decision modifies it.
Immutability — once merged, your contribution's rank is permanent. The network cannot rewrite history.
No recency bias — a sphere published two years ago competes equally with one published today. Reputation accumulates slowly and cannot be manufactured.
No central authority — rank is a pure ratio, computed on-chain from public data. No one decides who rises. The formula decides.
The system as a whole
Each formula attacks a different pathology of existing contribution systems:

Inflation / rushMiningTime in numerator, age in denominator

Manipulation / curationRankFrozen at merge, no retroactive change
Together they create a system where the only winning strategy is to participate genuinely, wait patiently, and contribute quality. There is no shortcut. There is no exploit. The protocol is the will of the network, formalized.

Theory
Toward a Compositional Theory of Participation
A bridge between information theory and the participation substrate
Starting point
In information theory, any system that produces sequences — a language model, a communication channel, a dynamic process — can be characterized by a single number: its entropy rate. This measures how much genuine uncertainty the system produces per step. A deterministic system has entropy rate zero. A purely random one has maximum entropy rate. Intelligence operates in between.
The Logarithmic Theory of Compositional Irreversibility (TLIC) applies this framework rigorously to language models — modeling them as Markov kernels in a category of probability spaces, with the entropy rate as the central invariant. It produces precise, falsifiable predictions about training dynamics, scaling laws, and phase transitions.
But TLIC describes an isolated module. It does not describe what happens when modules — human and machine — compose into a living network. That is the extension this note proposes.
1. What TLIC formalizes
TLIC establishes that a language model θ defines a Markov kernel K_θ: S ⇝ S in Kl(G). Under ergodicity and finite entropy, the Shannon entropy rate is a well-defined invariant — the Kolmogorov-Sinaï entropy of the system. It characterizes three regimes:
Contracting h(K) = 0 — deterministic attractor. The system converges.
Critical 0 < h(K) < log|S| — structured exploration. Intelligence operates here.
Diffusive h(K) = log|S| — uniform random walk. No macroscopic structure.
This is a complete theory of the isolated cognitive module. What it does not address is what happens when modules interact — when humans, language models, and spheres compose into a participation substrate.
2. The missing level
TLIC operates at the level of a single kernel. The participation substrate operates at a different level:
TLIC: token sequence → K_θ → next token Participation: participant_i ⊗ participant_j ⊗ modules → composed state
The relevant question is not "what is the entropy rate of this module?" but "what is the dimension of the configuration space opened by their composition?" This is not a refinement of TLIC. It is a different invariant, at a different level of the hierarchy.
3. The compositional invariant
Each sphere s ∈ Σ contributes an independent dimension to the configuration space through peerSection(s, i, j). Under orthogonality of sphere contributions:
n spheres → 2ⁿ configurations (expansion) n spheres → n options (amplification)
A system is an expander if and only if each sphere introduces a relational dimension not spanned by existing spheres.
The Hausdorff dimension of the configuration space dim_H(C(P,Σ)) is the formal counterpart of the distinction between diversity and expansion.
4. Human as Markov kernel
TLIC treats language models as Markov kernels. The participation theory requires treating humans as Markov kernels as well — not metaphorically, but structurally. A participant i defines a kernel K_i: Σ × Context → Actions. A participation substrate is then a composition of heterogeneous kernels:
K_substrate = K_θn ∘ K_im ∘ ... ∘ K_θ1 ∘ K_i1
The entropy rate of this composed system is well-defined, and characterizes the cognitive regime of the entire substrate. The intelligence of the substrate is not the sum of the entropy rates of its components — it is a property of the composed system.
5. Bifurcation and emergence
TLIC identifies grokking as a bifurcation of the stationary measure π_{K_θ}. The participation substrate exhibits an analogous phenomenon: when an orthogonal sphere is introduced, the stationary measure π_{K_substrate} bifurcates — the system reorganizes around new attractors structurally unreachable before.
Emergence is not a property of participation.
It is a property of the rewriting of the rules of participation themselves — a structural transition of the Markov kernel, not merely a distributional shift within a fixed kernel.
In TLIC terms: not a change in π_K but a change in K itself — the system evolves rather than grows.

Manifesto
Participation is All You Need
Toward distributed cognitive substrates beyond the transformer paradigm
1. The limits of attention
The transformer architecture demonstrated that a single mechanism — scaled attention over token sequences — could unify language understanding and generation. Eight years later, transformers dominate AI. Yet a structural tension has emerged in how they are typically deployed: increasingly centralized, stateless between sessions, and dependent on knowledge frozen at training time.
In most current product implementations, a transformer has no memory beyond its context window. Every session tends to begin from zero. The intelligence lives in data centers. Users, in practice, are treated as endpoints. These are not inevitable failures — they are consequences of design choices that made sense at scale, and that the participation paradigm proposes to complement.
2. What participation offers
Participation generates persistent state.
Participation distributes knowledge across time.
Participation decentralizes intelligence.
When a user participates in a network, structured behavioral signals accumulate into a living representation. This persists between sessions because it is not a context window — it is a profile that evolves.

4. Attention ≠ Participation
Most social networks, as currently designed, function less as participation systems and more as attention extraction systems. Their primary metric tends to be time on screen, clicks, engagement — not contribution, coordination, or emergence.
Social networks have largely failed to produce collective intelligence — not because it is impossible, but because their architecture was optimized for a different goal.
The productive opposition is not individual ↔ crowd. It is attention ↔ participation.
Intelligence as representation — the system builds a model and responds. Intelligence localized in weights. Benchmarkable.
Intelligence as dynamics — the system evolves toward better configurations. Intelligence in the trajectory. The question is not "what does it know?" but "where does it go?"
5. Collective reasoning is reorganization
participants → interactions → network reorganization → emergence
The result is not an answer. It is a transformation of the state of the system. A city reasons by reorganizing. A market converges toward a state. An ant colony does not compute the optimal path — the path appears.
We have spent eight years optimizing the individual cognitive mechanism — attention — and almost no work has been done on the social substrate in which cognition is embedded.
6. The four primitives
Persistent identity — recognizable across time by accumulated participation history, not by name.
Structured broadcast — participants emit structured signals about their current state to the network.
Composition — the intersection of two participants' states produces something neither held alone.
Incentive alignment — participation generates value for participants directly, rather than accruing it centrally.
7. The human is a component
In the current dominant architecture:
Human → AI → Response
The human is exterior to the cognitive system. Even "human-in-the-loop" frames the human as a correction mechanism, not a constitutive component. What the participation paradigm proposes:
Human ↔ Network ↔ Modules ↔ AI
The human is not merely a user of the system.
The human becomes a component of the system's dynamics.
The fundamental unit tends toward participation itself rather than the individual. Identity is not the input — identity emerges as output. A city is a movement. A language is a movement. YourMine aims to be a movement: the pattern persists as participants enter and leave.
The AI field tends to ask: how do we produce a better representation of the world?

The participation paradigm asks: how do we create the conditions for a world to self-organize?
8. Expansion, not amplification
Participation creates more participation if and only if it expands the configuration space of the system.
Without this condition, participation produces amplification — not emergence. And amplification of an existing pattern is not intelligence. It is repetition at scale.
Diversity — more variations within the same rules. Same interactions, same structures. Still amplification.
Expansion — new dimensions of rules become possible. Interactions that were structurally impossible before. The coordinate system changes.
n spheres → n options (amplification) n spheres → 2ⁿ configurations (expansion)
The composition primitive — peerSection() — converts the system from amplifier to expander. Without it, spheres are apps. With it, spheres are dimensions.
9. The open question
Emergence is not a property of participation.
It is a property of the rewriting of the rules of participation themselves.
The highest bar: not new interactions within existing rules — new rules that make previously impossible interactions possible. Participants can currently add spheres. They cannot yet change the grammar of participation itself. That is the frontier.
The system is running. The observation is ongoing. Whether this constitutes genuine emergence — in the sense of complex systems theory — remains an empirical question. But the test is clear: not whether participation increases. Whether new things become possible.
