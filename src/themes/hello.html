Toward distributed cognitive substrates beyond the transformer paradigm
1. The limits of attention
The transformer architecture demonstrated that a single mechanism — scaled attention over token sequences — could unify language understanding and generation. Eight years later, transformers dominate AI. Yet a structural tension has emerged in how they are typically deployed: increasingly centralized, stateless between sessions, and dependent on knowledge frozen at training time.
In most current product implementations, a transformer has no memory beyond its context window. Every session tends to begin from zero. The intelligence lives in data centers. Users, in practice, are treated as endpoints. These are not inevitable failures — they are consequences of design choices that made sense at scale, and that the participation paradigm proposes to complement.
2. What participation offers
Participation generates persistent state.
Participation distributes knowledge across time.
Participation decentralizes intelligence.
When a user participates in a network, structured behavioral signals accumulate into a living representation. This persists between sessions because it is not a context window — it is a profile that evolves.

4. Attention ≠ Participation
Most social networks, as currently designed, function less as participation systems and more as attention extraction systems. Their primary metric tends to be time on screen, clicks, engagement — not contribution, coordination, or emergence.
Social networks have largely failed to produce collective intelligence — not because it is impossible, but because their architecture was optimized for a different goal.
The productive opposition is not individual ↔ crowd. It is attention ↔ participation.
Intelligence as representation — the system builds a model and responds. Intelligence localized in weights. Benchmarkable.
Intelligence as dynamics — the system evolves toward better configurations. Intelligence in the trajectory. The question is not "what does it know?" but "where does it go?"
5. Collective reasoning is reorganization
participants → interactions → network reorganization → emergence
The result is not an answer. It is a transformation of the state of the system. A city reasons by reorganizing. A market converges toward a state. An ant colony does not compute the optimal path — the path appears.
We have spent eight years optimizing the individual cognitive mechanism — attention — and almost no work has been done on the social substrate in which cognition is embedded.
6. The four primitives
Persistent identity — recognizable across time by accumulated participation history, not by name.
Structured broadcast — participants emit structured signals about their current state to the network.
Composition — the intersection of two participants' states produces something neither held alone.
Incentive alignment — participation generates value for participants directly, rather than accruing it centrally.
7. The human is a component
In the current dominant architecture:
Human → AI → Response
The human is exterior to the cognitive system. Even "human-in-the-loop" frames the human as a correction mechanism, not a constitutive component. What the participation paradigm proposes:
Human ↔ Network ↔ Modules ↔ AI
The human is not merely a user of the system.
The human becomes a component of the system's dynamics.
The fundamental unit tends toward participation itself rather than the individual. Identity is not the input — identity emerges as output. A city is a movement. A language is a movement. YourMine aims to be a movement: the pattern persists as participants enter and leave.
The AI field tends to ask: how do we produce a better representation of the world?

The participation paradigm asks: how do we create the conditions for a world to self-organize?
8. Expansion, not amplification
Participation creates more participation if and only if it expands the configuration space of the system.
Without this condition, participation produces amplification — not emergence. And amplification of an existing pattern is not intelligence. It is repetition at scale.
Diversity — more variations within the same rules. Same interactions, same structures. Still amplification.
Expansion — new dimensions of rules become possible. Interactions that were structurally impossible before. The coordinate system changes.
n spheres → n options (amplification) n spheres → 2ⁿ configurations (expansion)
The composition primitive — peerSection() — converts the system from amplifier to expander. Without it, spheres are apps. With it, spheres are dimensions.
9. The open question
Emergence is not a property of participation.
It is a property of the rewriting of the rules of participation themselves.
The highest bar: not new interactions within existing rules — new rules that make previously impossible interactions possible. Participants can currently add spheres. They cannot yet change the grammar of participation itself. That is the frontier.
The system is running. The observation is ongoing. Whether this constitutes genuine emergence — in the sense of complex systems theory — remains an empirical question. But the test is clear: not whether participation increases. Whether new things become possible.

YourMine — Theoretical Foundation
What is a payoff?
Every theory of games assumes that what you win or lose can be expressed as a number. A real number. Something you can rank, compare, add. This assumption is so embedded it is rarely stated. It is just the definition of a payoff.
We argue this assumption fails for a class of systems — participatory, distributed, compositional — where what engagement produces is not a quantity but a structure. A relational object. Something that grows when you compose it with others and collapses to nothing when you withdraw.
The question is not how to generalize Nash equilibrium.
The question is what a payoff is.
The scalar assumption
Classical game theory requires two things: that every value can be assigned a number (representability), and that any two values can be compared (completeness). For markets and auctions, this works. Value is designed to be numerical there.
But participation produces something different. When two people share a sphere — a common context, a practice, a way of interacting — they produce a relational object. This object has structure. It can be extended. It can be partially ordered. It cannot be naturally reduced to a number without losing what makes it what it is.
Sen and incomplete preferences
Amartya Sen saw a related failure in welfare economics. He argued that forcing a complete ranking on incommensurable values — health, political freedom, social recognition — distorts the value structure rather than representing it. The agent who cannot rank more freedom against less health is not irrational. They are recognizing that the comparison the theory demands does not exist.
Sen proposed capability sets as a richer representation. But capability sets lack compositional structure — they do not combine when people interact strategically. There is no equilibrium theory for capability-valued games. That is the gap we address.
Categorically-valued open games
We extend open game theory (Ghani, Hedges, 2018) by replacing real-valued payoffs with objects in a symmetric monoidal category C. The classical case — a number — is recovered as the thin category induced by the total order on ℝ. The new framework admits payoffs that compose, that have an initial object (the empty relational structure produced by defection), and that are only partially ordered.
The defection object
In classical theory, defection produces a lower number — you still exist in the payoff space, just worse off. In categorically-valued theory, defection produces ∅C — the initial object, the empty structure. You are not worse off. You are outside the structure. This is the correct representation of what social defection actually produces.
Participatory equilibrium
A participatory equilibrium is a strategy profile where no player has a strictly dominant deviation — no unilateral change produces a payoff strictly above their current one in the partial order. Because the order is partial, incomparable deviations are not improvements. They are just different. Incomparability is native to the definition.
Incomparability is not a bug
Incomparability is what value looks like when it is structure rather than quantity.
Classical theory treats incomparable outcomes as a theoretical embarrassment — a sign that the model is incomplete, that a common scale has not yet been found. The entire project of welfare economics is in part a project of finding ways to compare the incomparable.
We propose the opposite reading. Two participation patterns that produce qualitatively different relational structures are not better or worse than each other. They are different in kind. A framework that forces comparison loses information. A framework that represents incomparability faithfully preserves it.
The value sequence
The viscoelastic theory of value (L'Œil et la Main, ch. 4) provides a physical grounding for this framework. Value is not assigned to objects from outside. It emerges from a generative metabolic process:
Elasticity → Velocity → Liquidity → Utility → Value
The scalar payoff of classical game theory is the terminal state of this sequence — value as already-produced quantity, abstracted from the process that generated it. Categorical payoffs represent states in the generative sequence, not just its output.
YourMine as instance
The identity surface of user A as seen by user B — produced by composing the contributions of all spheres they share — is an object in the category of diagrams. It is the empty diagram when they share nothing. It grows under composition. It is partially ordered by inclusion. Two users with different shared sphere configurations produce incomparable identity surfaces: neither participation pattern dominates the other. They are qualitatively different.
No central authority — because the context is produced by the players, not assigned from outside.
No fixed identity — because identity is the colimit of all shared sphere compositions.
No universal ranking — because participation patterns are incomparable in the partial order.
A composable object is not a generalization of a number.
It is a different answer to a different question.
